The Calibration Program.
Two studies on what AI is actually doing
to executive judgment.

The work that funds the opinions.

Most claims about AI and leadership are anecdote. We're running the empirical studies anyway. Two pre-registered investigations of how generative AI assistance is changing prioritization at Fortune 500 scale — one at the level of the individual executive, one at the level of the organizational portfolio.

Why we're running this

In the last twenty-four months, every F500 organization has put generative AI into the hands of its leaders. Vendors claim it makes decisions faster, sharper, more data-informed. Critics claim it erodes judgment, inflates confidence, and floods portfolios with low-quality work. Both camps are arguing from anecdote.

The Calibration Program is an attempt to measure the actual effect — at two levels of analysis, with pre-registered hypotheses, blinded outcome scoring, and public methodology. We are not running it because we know the answer. We are running it because nobody does, and because the answer changes how every operator we advise should think about AI rollouts.

Both studies are open. We publish the methodology before the data, the data before the analysis, and the analysis before any opinion piece. Pre-registrations will go to OSF (Open Science Framework). Anonymized datasets will be released under CC-BY.

Study 01 · The micro question

The Confidence Inflation Hypothesis.

Does AI assistance make Fortune 500 executives more confident in their prioritization decisions without making those decisions better — and how large is the gap?

Hypothesis
H1 · Pre-registered AI-assisted prioritization will raise stated confidence by at least 25% (vs. unaided baseline) while improving decision quality, scored 90 days later by a blind expert panel, by less than 5%. The gap between confidence lift and accuracy lift is the calibration debt.
Method
  • 80–120 directors and VPs across 20+ F500 companies, recruited through partner organizations and the NS Studio research network.
  • Each participant makes three real prioritization decisions drawn from their actual current portfolio — one unaided, one AI-assisted, one with a human peer reviewer (control). Order is randomized, decisions are de-identified.
  • Stated confidence captured immediately after each decision on a calibrated 0–100 scale.
  • Decision quality scored at 30 days (early signal) and 90 days (outcome) by a blind expert panel of three independent operators per case.
  • Blind scoring — panelists do not know which condition produced which decision.

Study 02 · The macro question

Priority Drift Under AI.

Does adopting generative AI tools accelerate the decay rate of stated F500 strategic priorities — that is, does AI make companies more scattered over time, not less?

Hypothesis
H2 · Pre-registered F500 companies with above-median internal AI adoption will show priority half-lives 2–3× shorter than peer companies with below-median adoption, controlling for industry, size, and CEO tenure. AI lowers the cost of starting things — and that creates portfolio sprawl, not focus.
Method
  • Five-year corpus of F500 quarterly earnings call transcripts (public), coded for stated strategic priorities using a fine-tuned classifier with human validation.
  • Priority half-life calculated per priority per company: how many quarters until a stated priority disappears from official communication.
  • AI adoption signals derived from public sources — job postings (LinkedIn, BLS), 10-K mentions of AI tooling, public vendor partnership announcements, internal AI policy filings.
  • Matched-pair analysis: pre-AI-adoption period vs post-AI-adoption period within the same company; cross-company comparisons across matched industry and size cohorts.
  • Confound controls: CEO tenure, M&A activity, macroeconomic shocks, industry-wide priority shifts.

Questions we're still wrestling with.

We are publishing these openly because we don't have clean answers yet. If any of them are your problem — write to us. The program is better with more critics.

How do we operationalize "decision quality" in a way that doesn't collapse into hindsight bias when scored 90 days out?

Do we measure confidence as point-in-time stated confidence, or as posterior calibration (predicted vs. observed outcome distributions)?

How do we handle the selection effect where the leaders most willing to participate may already be the most calibration-aware?

Should the F500 corpus include full Russell 1000 to widen the comparison set, or stay strictly F500 to preserve sample homogeneity?

Is "stated priority" on an earnings call a clean enough construct, given that earnings calls are themselves a performance for analysts?

What's the right way to share intermediate findings without anchoring participants in later cohorts?
