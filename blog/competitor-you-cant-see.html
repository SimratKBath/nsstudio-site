The AI competitor you can't see: until you lose to it.

The most dangerous competitor isn't another AI vendor. It's the behavior already in place.

When teams map their AI competitors, they list other AI vendors. That's the easy mapping. Their pricing pages, their feature matrices, their funding announcements, easy to find, easy to compare.

But the company that quietly eats most AI products isn't a vendor. It's the existing behavior, the spreadsheet, the ChatGPT tab, the colleague-Slack, the "we just deal with it." We call this the invisible incumbent. It has 100% market share, it charges nothing, and switching away from it is harder than switching from any product.

Why the invisible incumbent wins so often.

Three reasons. They compound.

One: it's already in the user's habit loop. When a user does X, they reflexively reach for tool Y. The reflex is fast and free. Your AI feature has to break that reflex before it can replace it. The default in human behavior is "what I did last time." Your feature is competing against muscle memory.

Two: the user doesn't perceive a switch. When a competitor product asks the user to switch, the user perceives a switch, they'll evaluate, compare, weigh. When the invisible incumbent is the alternative, switching feels like extra work for unclear benefit. The user isn't comparing two products; they're comparing "do something new" vs "keep doing what works."

Three: the invisible incumbent is good enough. The reason it's the incumbent is that, despite being clunky, it gets the job done. ChatGPT plus a copy-paste workflow produces 80% of the value your AI feature would, at zero integration cost. Your feature has to be 5–10x better, not 1.5x.

The mapping exercise we run with clients.

For every proposed AI feature, we ask:

  1. What is the user doing right now to solve this problem?
  2. How long does it take them?
  3. What part of it annoys them most?
  4. What part of it works fine?
  5. If we removed our AI feature tomorrow, how would they replace it?

The answers determine whether you have a real opening. If the user is doing nothing, there's no problem. If the user has a workflow that mostly works, the bar is high. If the user is actively cobbling together a solution out of three tools, that's the painkiller signal you want.

Differentiation only matters relative to the user's current behavior. Most AI feature decks are differentiated against the wrong baseline.

What to do when the invisible incumbent is too strong.

Sometimes the answer is: don't build. The user's current behavior is good enough, and your AI feature would be marginally better at higher cost. This is the most underrated answer in AI strategy. We tell clients this regularly.

When you do see a real opening, the play is usually one of two:

Replace the worst part of the current behavior. Don't try to replace the whole workflow, replace the specific moment the user complains about. Then expand from there. AI features that try to replace entire workflows almost always underdeliver. Features that replace the worst 20% of an existing workflow stick.

Become invisible yourself. The strongest AI features don't ask for attention. They live inside the user's existing tool, fire automatically when needed, and stay quiet the rest of the time. Your AI competing against the invisible incumbent has a chance only when it's at least equally invisible.

The diagnostic question.

Before your next AI build decision, ask: What does the user do today, and why is that not enough?

If you can answer the second part with a specific painful moment that costs the user something measurable, go build. If you can't, you don't have a competitor; you have a vibe. Vibes don't ship.
