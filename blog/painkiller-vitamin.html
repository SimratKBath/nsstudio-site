Is your AI product a painkiller or a vitamin?

The one test that separates AI features people actually use from features that demo well and quietly die.

Paul Graham has a framework for customer discovery: you're either building a painkiller or a vitamin. Painkillers solve problems people feel daily and pay to make stop. Vitamins are nice-to-haves people approve of in surveys and never use in practice.

Most AI features are vitamins dressed as painkillers. They demo beautifully — the AI summarizes, generates, suggests — and then users glance at the output, nod, and go back to what they were doing. The feature isn't bad. It's just not load-bearing.

The test.

Ask three real users this question: "If this AI feature disappeared tomorrow, what specifically would you do differently?"

If the answer is one of these, it's a vitamin:

  • "I'd miss it, but I'd be fine."
  • "I'd use ChatGPT instead."
  • "Nothing really, I forgot about it."
  • "I don't use it that much anyway."

If the answer sounds like one of these, it's a painkiller:

  • "I'd lose two hours of my day."
  • "I'd have to hire someone."
  • "I'd miss errors I now catch automatically."
  • "I literally can't go back."

Three users is enough. The pattern is unmistakable in either direction. We've never run this test and gotten ambiguous results.

Why this matters more for AI than other features.

AI features have a unique pathology: they impress in demos and underperform in production. The reason is that demos are optimized for novelty (look what it can do!) and production is optimized for habit (does this fit my actual workflow?). Most AI features cross the demo line and never cross the habit line.

Demos sell capability. Production buys habit. Most AI features sell what they can't deliver.

The pre-build version of the test.

Before building, you can run a sharper version of the test by asking your prospective users this: "Walk me through the last time you experienced [the problem the AI feature solves]. Tell me what you actually did."

If they can recall a specific instance from the last week — that's a painkiller candidate. If they have to think hard, or describe the problem in the abstract, or admit the problem doesn't actually come up that much — vitamin.

The error founders make: they ask "would you use this?" and users politely say yes. Always. The painkiller test asks about behavior in the past tense, not preferences in the future tense. The two answers diverge wildly.

What to do when you discover you've built a vitamin.

You have three options, in order of preference:

  1. Find the painkiller adjacent to the vitamin. Often the AI capability is right but the problem framing is wrong. Talk to users about their actual painful moments. Is there one your existing tech could address with a different interface?
  2. Kill it cleanly. Most companies can't bring themselves to do this. They keep the feature on life support because it cost money to build. Sunk cost is a tax. Pay it once and move on.
  3. Reframe as ambient infrastructure. Sometimes a vitamin works as plumbing — invisible, always-on, providing small lifts everywhere. This is rare and usually a rationalization. Use sparingly.

Whichever option you choose, name it explicitly. Vitamins that live as not-quite-killed half-features are the silent budget killer in most AI portfolios.

The corollary: not every business needs a painkiller AI feature.

Some businesses don't have a painkiller-shaped AI opportunity. Their workflows are already efficient. Their users aren't in acute pain. The honest answer is: no AI feature, yet. Wait until the technology produces a 10x lift in something you actually care about, then revisit.

We've told paying clients this. They occasionally object — they hired us to find an AI strategy, not to be told there isn't a good one. But the alternative is helping them build a vitamin they'll regret. The fee is small compared to the year of opportunity cost.
