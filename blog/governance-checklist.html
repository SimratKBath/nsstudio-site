The minimum viable AI governance kit.

The smallest set of policies, evals, and cadences that keep AI from quietly becoming a compliance fire — designed for ops leaders, not legal teams.

Most AI governance documents are written by lawyers and read by nobody. They are long, careful, and operationally useless. They describe principles ("the company uses AI responsibly") instead of decisions ("model X is approved for use case Y, reviewed quarterly").

The version below is the opposite. It's an operational kit you can stand up in two afternoons. It won't make your legal team happy on its own — for that, you'll layer the formal policy on top — but it's what actually prevents AI from quietly becoming a problem in your company.

Component 1: The decision rights map.

One page. Three columns. For each AI use case, name:

  • The owner (who decides whether to deploy)
  • The reviewer (who has to sign off before deployment)
  • The escalation path (what triggers re-review)

Example: "Customer support chatbot. Owner: VP Support. Reviewer: VP Eng + Legal. Escalation: any change to model, training data, or accuracy threshold under 95%."

That's it. One row per use case. Most companies have three to five rows. The map fits on a single page and lives in a known location. When something happens, the first thing anyone does is check the map. The map answers the question.

Component 2: The model approval list.

Which AI models are approved for which use cases? Companies that skip this end up with engineers using arbitrary models, vendors changing terms unnoticed, and data flowing through services nobody audited.

The list has four columns: Model, Vendor, Approved use cases, Last reviewed. Reviewed quarterly. Out-of-list use requires approval per the decision rights map.

The first time a vendor changes their privacy policy and you didn't notice is the last time you'll skip this list.

Component 3: The eval framework.

For every AI use case in production, you need a specific, behavioral metric for whether it's working. Not "is the AI good." Specific.

Examples:

  • Customer support: % of conversations escalated. Threshold: under 12%.
  • Sales qualification: false-negative rate (qualified leads marked unqualified). Threshold: under 5%.
  • Internal research summarization: human-rated factual accuracy on a sampled set of 50 queries per quarter. Threshold: 90%.

The threshold is what triggers escalation. The sample-set is what makes it measurable. Without both, you don't have an eval — you have a feeling.

Component 4: The monthly review cadence.

Forty minutes, last Friday of the month. Three agenda items:

  1. Walk the decision rights map. Anything change? Anything new added? (10 min)
  2. Walk the model approval list. Any vendor changes? Any new models requested? (10 min)
  3. Walk the eval results. Any threshold breached? Any pattern emerging? (20 min)

Three people in the room: the AI owner (often a VP of Eng or Ops), one engineering rep, and one business rep. Notes filed in a known place. Done.

That's the entire monthly cadence. About forty minutes per month. The companies we work with that skip this end up with quarterly fire drills. The companies that do this never have fire drills.

Component 5: The escalation runbook.

When something goes wrong, what happens in the first hour? Write that down once. Format:

  • Trigger: Eval threshold breach / customer complaint / regulator inquiry / vendor incident
  • First call: AI owner
  • Second call: Reviewer pair (per the decision rights map)
  • Holding action: What gets paused or rolled back while we investigate
  • Communication template: Pre-drafted note for affected users / customers

The first hour after a problem is where most damage is done. Pre-writing this runbook means the first hour is calm and procedural instead of panicked and improvised.

What this kit replaces — and what it doesn't.

It replaces: the bloated 30-page AI policy that nobody reads. The vague principles. The "we have governance" fig leaf.

It doesn't replace: formal compliance work for regulated industries (HIPAA, FERPA, GDPR specifics, SOC 2 attestations). For those, layer the formal policy on top of this operational kit. The kit ensures the formal policy is actually followed in practice.

How to stand it up in two afternoons.

Afternoon 1. Write the decision rights map and the model approval list. Get one engineering lead and one business lead in the room. List every AI use case you know about. For each: pick owner, reviewer, escalation. List every model in use: which use case, last reviewed.

Afternoon 2. Write the eval framework, monthly cadence, and escalation runbook. The hardest part is forcing specificity on the evals. Don't accept "the AI works well." Get to numbers.

By the end of week one, the entire kit lives in a shared document. It's reviewed monthly. It evolves. It saves the company from fires that haven't happened yet — and that's the point.
